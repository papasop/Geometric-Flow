\begin{abstract}
\noindent
A fixed quantum task can admit continuous families of physically distinct
implementations with the same ideal endpoint, yet those implementations need not
respond equally to noise or optimization. We formulate a local
\emph{stable--neutral implementation geometry}: endpoint-preserving tangent
directions are neutral, while transverse response is governed by a
positive-definite, generally non-diagonal normal operator $A$.
In an exact density-matrix construction cross-checked against Qiskit/Aer, random-direction trajectory
data recover the full operator without seeding its eigendirections, with
$2.62\%$ relative Frobenius error and eigendirection alignment
$>0.99999999$. The recovered eigenvalues agree with independently fitted
impulse-decay rates to within $2.8\%$. Freezing the quadratic-fit operator before evaluation
yields $0.54\%$ mean vector error on a held-out sustained-load protocol, whereas scalar,
coordinate-diagonal, and spectrum-preserving eigendirection-scrambled reductions perform
$91$--$111$ times worse. Under
isotropic driving, the same operator reproduces the stationary covariance to
$1.08\%$, including cross-covariance generated by the non-axis-aligned response.
We then invert the operator as a geometric preconditioner. Across twenty random
starts, $A^{-1}$ reaches the same local optimum with $78.5$ mean function
evaluations, compared with $165.0$--$201.5$ for simplified or ordinary flows.
Under an equal $180$-evaluation budget, it leaves a residual normal
displacement orders of magnitude smaller, reaching the numerical floor of this
near-quadratic landscape.
These results provide a constructive proof of principle that the relevant local
robustness object can be a direction-resolved operator rather than a scalar
score: the same $A$ can be identified from response data, used predictively on an
unseen protocol, and inverted computationally. Additional exact-simulation and
PASQAL Cloud \code{EMU\_FREE} studies provide supporting robustness-landscape
evidence. No physical-QPU validation or universal claim that computation is
geometric flow is made.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A quantum task is usually specified by a circuit, pulse sequence, or target
unitary. Yet a fixed ideal task endpoint can often be realized by continuously
many physically distinct implementations. These implementations are equivalent
in the noiseless task sense, but need not be equivalent under coherent drift,
stochastic exposure, calibration error, or numerical optimization~\cite{preskill2018}. The natural
object is therefore not only a single circuit, but the family of implementations
that realize the same ideal endpoint.

Most robustness descriptions compress this family to a scalar quantity: gate
count, total duration, fidelity, sensitivity, or a single effective decay rate.
Such summaries can be insufficient when perturbations in different control
directions are coupled. A scalar cannot encode rotated soft and stiff directions,
nor can a coordinate-diagonal model represent response mixing between parameters.
This motivates a direction-resolved local description of implementation
robustness.

We model a locally smooth set of task-endpoint-equivalent implementations as an
implementation manifold. Near a locally stable--neutral point, deformations
tangent to the endpoint-equivalent set leave the ideal endpoint unchanged and
are neutral to leading order in the chosen noisy loss, while transverse
deformations experience positive restoring curvature. The resulting transverse
response is governed by a normal operator
\[
  A=P_N H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}P_N ,
\]
defined with respect to an explicit parameter-space metric. The operator is a
local curvature-response object on implementation space; it is not a Hamiltonian,
a Lindbladian, or a generator of physical hardware time.

The central contribution of this work is to make this operator operational. In a
controlled exact density-matrix construction, we:
\[
  \text{identify }A
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  \text{predict with }A
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  \text{compute with }A^{-1}.
\]
First, the full non-diagonal operator is recovered through a second estimation route
using random-direction trajectory data without seeding its eigendirections. Second, a
frozen operator predicts a held-out sustained-load protocol, while scalar,
coordinate-diagonal, and eigendirection-scrambled reductions fail strongly.
Third, the inverse operator acts as a geometric preconditioner, reducing the
number of objective evaluations required to reach the same optimum and returning
solutions substantially closer to the endpoint-equivalent manifold under a fixed
budget.

The broader robustness-landscape studies serve as supporting operational
evidence. Endpoint-equivalent echo families exhibit a non-monotone noisy
landscape in which coherent-drift cancellation competes with stochastic
exposure; pure search discovers non-shortest focused paths, continuous flows
concentrate near the focused basin, and the basin collapses under no-drift,
reverse-flow, and shuffled controls. Additional GHZ$_3$ recovery and PASQAL
Cloud \code{EMU\_FREE} experiments test related path-dependent robustness effects.
The \textsc{bb1} sequence is used only as a known robustness landmark
\cite{wimperis1994}, not as a new pulse-design contribution.

\paragraph{Claim hierarchy.}
The principal claim is local and constructive: within the exact density-matrix realization cross-checked against Qiskit/Aer,
a full normal operator is identified, validated across response protocols, and
used as a preconditioner. The path-landscape, GHZ$_3$, and PASQAL Cloud EMU
studies support the broader implementation-geometry picture but do not establish
the operator result independently. A universal interpretation of computation as
geometric flow remains a hypothesis.

Table~\ref{tab:evidence-ledger} summarizes the evidence hierarchy and separates
the three claim-carrying operator results from the supporting landscape studies.

\paragraph{Two distinct objectives.}
The paper uses two noninterchangeable losses. The path objective
\[
J_{\mathrm{path}}
=
\mathrm{TVD}_{\mathrm{mit}}
+\lambda_{\mathrm{cost}}\hat c
+\Pi_{\mathrm{end}}
\]
scores complete implementation paths in the supporting benchmarks. The local
objective $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$, instantiated as a noisy single-qubit loss in the
constructed realization, defines the normal Hessian and operator $A$. Thus
$A$ is not the Hessian of the $54$-case path-search objective. The paper first develops
and tests the local operator, and then presents the supporting landscape and
cloud-emulation evidence.

\section{Geometric Formulation}\label{sec:geom}

We first formulate the local, direction-resolved geometry needed for the principal
normal-operator claim. Rather than a single scalar robustness score, we separate three
notions that such a score would conflate---\emph{endpoint equivalence}, \emph{tangent
neutrality}, and \emph{normal robustness}---and replace the qualitative
``attracting-basin'' picture by an operator that predicts rates and directions of
restoration. The complementary path-landscape framework, which scores whole
implementation paths, is introduced later with the supporting evidence
(Section~\ref{sec:framework}).


\subsection{Endpoint-equivalent implementation sets}
Let $E:\Theta\to\mathcal P$ be the ideal (noiseless) task-endpoint map, sending an
implementation parameter $\theta$ to its ideal output distribution. For a fixed
target $E_\star$ the endpoint-equivalent set is
\[
  \mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}=\{\theta:\ E(\theta)=E_\star\}.
\]
Within a locally smooth, constant-rank neighborhood $\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ is
modeled as a manifold whose tangent space is the kernel of the endpoint differential,
\[
  T_\theta\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}=\ker DE(\theta),
\]
with $N_\theta\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ the complementary normal space. No global
smoothness, connectedness, or single-manifold structure is assumed;
$\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ may be disconnected, bounded, or stratified.


\subsection{Stable--neutral decomposition}
Endpoint equivalence alone does \emph{not} imply that the noisy loss is flat along
$\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$: $E(\theta)=E_\star$ is a noiseless identity, whereas the
noisy loss $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$ is a distinct object. We therefore do not call the whole
endpoint-equivalent set stable--neutral. Instead, writing $P_T,P_N$ for the
orthogonal projectors onto $T_\theta\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ and
$N_\theta\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$, we define a \emph{stable--neutral} point locally.

\begin{quote}
An endpoint-equivalent family is called \emph{locally stable--neutral} at
$\bar\theta\in\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ when, to leading order in the loss
$J_{\mathrm{loc}}$,
\[
  P_T\,\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}}(\bar\theta)\approx 0,\qquad
  P_T\,H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\bar\theta)\,P_T\approx 0,\qquad
  P_N\,H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\bar\theta)\,P_N\ \succ\ 0 .
\]
The stable--neutral subset $\mathcal M_{\mathrm{sn}}\subseteq\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$
is the locus of such points, and
$A_{\bar\theta}:=P_N H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\bar\theta)P_N$ is the \emph{normal response
operator}.
\end{quote}

These conditions are logically distinct: endpoint equivalence is noiseless, whereas
tangent neutrality and normal robustness concern $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$. Neither property is
assumed globally; both are tested only in the constructed local realization
(Section~\ref{sec:construct}).


\subsection{Two objectives: $J_{\mathrm{path}}$ and $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$}\label{sec:twoobj}
We use two distinct objectives. The path objective
$J_{\mathrm{path}}=\mathrm{TVD}_{\mathrm{mit}}+\lambda_{\mathrm{cost}}\hat c+\Pi_{\mathrm{end}}$
scores whole implementation paths in the supporting benchmarks (Section~\ref{sec:support}),
whereas the local objective $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$ (a single-qubit noisy loss $1-P(0)$)
defines the local normal Hessian in the constructed realization of
Sections~\ref{sec:construct}--\ref{sec:compute}. Accordingly $A\neq\nabla^2 J_{\mathrm{path}}$:
the normal operator is not the Hessian of the $54$-case path-search objective.


\subsection{Linear normal-response model}
Write the normal-space displacement as $n\in N_\theta\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$.
Linearizing implementation-space descent about $\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ gives an
overdamped local response
\begin{equation}
  \dot n=-A\,n+f+\eta,\qquad \operatorname{Cov}(\eta\,ds)=Q\,ds,
  \label{eq:normal-linear}
\end{equation}
where $A=P_N H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}P_N$ is the normal response operator, $f$ a sustained
forcing (a transverse ``load''), and $\eta$ a driving fluctuation with a
\emph{prescribed} covariance $Q=BB^\top$ that is in general anisotropic and
correlated (it is not assumed isotropic). The evolution variable is an
\emph{implementation-space flow parameter} $s$, not physical time:
\[
  s\neq t_{\mathrm{hardware}},\qquad s\neq d\tau,\qquad s\neq dt_{\mathrm{info}}.
\]
The flow is an optimization flow defined by the chosen descent rule
$\dot n=-\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}}$, linearized about the manifold; it is \emph{not} an
independently observed physical relaxation process, and $A$ is a curvature operator on
parameter space, not a Hamiltonian or Lindbladian generator. The linear model
\eqref{eq:normal-linear} has three consequences that we test \emph{separately} (and
at different evidence strength) in Section~\ref{sec:predict}:
\begin{align}
  \text{transient restoration:}\quad & n(s)=e^{-As}\,n(0),\\
  \text{static susceptibility:}\quad & n_\infty=A^{-1}f,\\
  \text{stationary fluctuations:}\quad & A\Sigma+\Sigma A^\top=Q .
\end{align}

\paragraph{Choice of parameter metric.} Treating the Hessian as an \emph{operator}
$A=P_N H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}P_N$ and forming $A^{-1}$ implicitly uses an inner product on
parameter space: under a general coordinate change a Hessian transforms as a bilinear
form, and raising it to a linear operator requires a metric. Throughout the local
construction the normal coordinates are equipped with the Euclidean parameter metric
$g=I$; accordingly, $A$ denotes the metric-raised normal Hessian in this chosen
coordinate chart. The preconditioning advantage below is therefore established for this
explicit parameterization and metric; coordinate-invariant or natural-metric
generalizations are left for future work. (The full/diagonal/rotated ablations already
show that \emph{direction} information matters beyond a global rescaling, but the
coordinate dependence of $A$ itself is stated here explicitly.)

\section{Constructed Local Realization and Identification of $A$}\label{sec:construct}

This section constructs the local exact density-matrix (V2--V5.1) realization of the stable--neutral
geometry of Section~\ref{sec:geom} and identifies the normal response operator $A$.
Prediction and preconditioning are treated in Sections~\ref{sec:predict}
and~\ref{sec:compute}. Throughout, $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$ is the local noisy loss of this
constructed realization, not the $54$-case path objective $J_{\mathrm{path}}$. The
numerical values reported in this and the next two sections are from the
self-contained reproduction run
(\code{geometric\_flow\_master.py}), using an exact density-matrix propagator
cross-checked against Qiskit/Aer. The author's earlier exact-Aer result files are
included in the reproduction bundle as a separately implemented historical comparison; they
reproduce the same qualitative ordering and structural conclusions, although some numerical
effect sizes differ substantially (Supplement). Figure~\ref{fig:spine}
previews the whole spine $\text{identify }A\to\text{predict with }A\to\text{compute with }A^{-1}$.

\subsection{Circuit and local loss}
The normal-operator results (Section~\ref{sec:construct}) use a separate constructed
local realization with its own loss $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$, distinct from the path
benchmarks above. We summarize the protocol so it is reproducible; exact seeds, grids,
and per-optimizer settings accompany the reproduction bundle.

A single-qubit exact density-matrix construction defines $J_{\mathrm{loc}}=1-P(0)$ on a
two-dimensional normal space. We estimate $A$ both from a local quadratic fit and,
independently, from random-direction descent trajectories. The frozen operator is then
tested on impulse decay, held-out forcing, covariance closure, and four optimization
flows, including three preconditioned variants. Complete numerical settings are listed in the Supplement
(Table~\ref{tab:supp-hparams}).

% =====================================================================

\subsection{Tangent neutrality}
Sampling endpoint-equivalent deformations \emph{along} $\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$,
both the ideal endpoint and the local noisy loss are numerically invariant:
\[
  \max_{\tau\in\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}}\mathrm{TVD}_{\mathrm{ideal}}=1.110\times10^{-16},
  \qquad
  \operatorname{range}_{\tau\in\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}}J_{\mathrm{loc}}=1.110\times10^{-16}.
\]
Tangent deformations therefore leave both the ideal task endpoint and the local
noisy loss unchanged to machine precision, witnessing
$P_T\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}}\approx0$ with $T_\theta\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ in the
near-kernel of $H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}$. This is \emph{tangent neutrality}, distinct
from endpoint equivalence (a noiseless identity) and from normal robustness
(established next); we keep the three concepts separate rather than merging them into
one ``robustness'' number.


\subsection{Quadratic normal operator}
A local quadratic fit of $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$ on the two-dimensional normal space yields
\[
  A_{\mathrm{quad}}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0.68818381 & 0.44776476\\[2pt]
    0.44776476 & 1.16434530
  \end{pmatrix},
\]
with eigenvalues and eigenvectors
\[
  \lambda_1=0.41913960,\quad \lambda_2=1.43338951,\qquad
  e_1=\begin{pmatrix}-0.8571673\\[1pt] 0.5150381\end{pmatrix},\quad
  e_2=\begin{pmatrix}0.5150381\\[1pt] 0.8571673\end{pmatrix},
\]
and off-diagonal strength
\[
  \frac{\lvert A_{12}\rvert}{\sqrt{A_{11}A_{22}}}=0.500 .
\]
The restoring eigendirections do \emph{not} coincide with the parameter coordinate
axes. Robustness is therefore not a collection of per-parameter local
sensitivities but a genuinely coupled normal operator: the transverse response mixes
parameters, and the natural stiff and soft directions are rotated relative to the
coordinate frame (Figure~\ref{fig:identify}a).


\subsection{Random-direction dynamic identification}\label{sec:indep}
To identify the operator without using Hessian eigendirections as initialization
directions, we launch transverse perturbations from random normal-space directions and
evolve them using finite-difference gradients evaluated directly from the
same exact density-matrix loss used in the reproduction pipeline. The full
linear propagator is then fitted from the resulting trajectories, and its matrix
logarithm gives the continuous generator. This yields
\[
  A_{\mathrm{dyn}}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0.70116211 & 0.46337181\\[2pt]
    0.46337181 & 1.19387320
  \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad\text{compared with}\qquad
  A_{\mathrm{Hess}}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0.68818381 & 0.44776476\\[2pt]
    0.44776476 & 1.16434530
  \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The relative Frobenius error $\lVert A_{\mathrm{dyn}}-A_{\mathrm{Hess}}\rVert_F/\lVert A_{\mathrm{Hess}}\rVert_F$ is $2.62\%$. The dynamically recovered eigenvalues differ
from the Hessian eigenvalues by $0.86\%$ and $2.72\%$, while the absolute
eigendirection alignments are $0.999999998$ for both modes. Random-direction
trajectory data therefore recover, within the same constructed loss model, both the
spectrum and the principal axes of the local normal geometry---the eigendirection
agreement is a genuine prediction, not an artifact of directional initialization. This
identification relies on the explicitly chosen Euclidean parameter metric and
gradient-flow convention $\dot{\mathbf n}=-\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}}$ (Section~\ref{sec:geom});
under another metric the corresponding linear response operator would be the metric-raised
Hessian rather than the coordinate Hessian alone.

\section{Predicting Response with $A$}\label{sec:predict}

\subsection{Impulse-decay rates}
An impulse-recovery experiment---displacing the implementation transverse to
$\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$ along the fitted Hessian eigendirections and measuring the
return under implementation-space flow---fits decay rates
\[
  \hat\lambda_1=0.422944,\qquad \hat\lambda_2=1.472805,
\]
along $n_i(s)\approx n_i(0)\,e^{-\lambda_i s}$, where $s$ is the implementation-space
flow parameter ($s\neq t_{\mathrm{hardware}}$, $s\neq d\tau$, $s\neq dt_{\mathrm{info}}$).
These match the normal-Hessian eigenvalues to relative errors
\[
  0.908\%\ (\lambda_1),\qquad 2.750\%\ (\lambda_2)
\]
(Figure~\ref{fig:predict}a). The curvature operator therefore predicts the
\emph{rates} of restoration. Because these impulses are initialized along the Hessian
eigendirections, this test validates the decay rates rather than independently identifying
the axes; the latter is supplied by the random-direction reconstruction of
Section~\ref{sec:indep} (eigendirection alignment $>0.99999999$).


\subsection{Held-out sustained-load prediction}
We now \emph{freeze} the quadratic-fit normal operator
\[
  A_{\mathrm{frozen}}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0.68818381 & 0.44776476\\[2pt]
    0.44776476 & 1.16434530
  \end{pmatrix},
\]
and, without any refitting, predict the steady displacement under \emph{held-out}
sustained forcing directions $f$ that did not enter the identification, via
$n_\infty^{\mathrm{pred}}=A_{\mathrm{frozen}}^{-1}f$. On the held-out load set the
full-operator vector error is
\[
  \mathrm{mean}=0.000189,\qquad \mathrm{median}=0.000189,\qquad \mathrm{max}=0.000313,
\]
i.e.\ about $0.54\%$ mean and $0.90\%$ maximum error normalized to the mean load. This is the \emph{primary
cross-protocol prediction} of the paper: the operator identified from local response data
generalizes to a held-out sustained-load protocol it was never fitted on, rather
than fitting the same data twice. It is stronger than basin attraction, which only asserts
descent toward a minimum.

\paragraph{Operator variants.} Two estimation procedures enter this section. The
quadratic-fit operator $A_{\mathrm{quad}}$---identical, in this reproduction, to the
finite-difference normal Hessian $A_{\mathrm{Hess}}$ and to the frozen operator
$A_{\mathrm{frozen}}$ used for held-out prediction, since all three come from the same
local fit---is one estimate; the eigendirection-unseeded operator $A_{\mathrm{dyn}}$
reconstructed from random-direction trajectories is the other. Both are estimates of the
same local normal operator $A=P_N H_{J_{\mathrm{loc}}}P_N$ and differ by $2.62\%$ in
relative Frobenius norm (referenced to $\lVert A_{\mathrm{Hess}}\rVert_F$, as in
Section~\ref{sec:construct}); they are not treated as distinct physical operators. The
held-out prediction, the covariance closure (Section~\ref{sec:covclosure}), and the V3/V4
preconditioning experiments (Section~\ref{sec:compute}) all use this single fitted operator
$A_{\mathrm{quad}}$, with scalar and coordinate-diagonal reductions constructed from that
same matrix---the operator produced by the in-bundle reproduction pipeline.


\subsection{Scalar, diagonal, and rotated ablations}
To show the full operator---both eigenvalues \emph{and} eigendirections---is doing the
work, we replace it by three matched simplifications and re-score the same held-out
loads. $E_{\mathrm{scalar}}$ replaces $A$ by a single robustness rate,
$E_{\mathrm{diagonal}}$ keeps only the coordinate-axis entries, and
$E_{\mathrm{rotated}}$ preserves the eigenvalue spectrum but scrambles the
eigendirections:
\[
  E_{\mathrm{full}}=0.000189,\quad
  E_{\mathrm{diagonal}}=0.017251,\quad
  E_{\mathrm{scalar}}=0.019067,\quad
  E_{\mathrm{rotated}}=0.020929,
\]
with relative disadvantages
\[
  \frac{E_{\mathrm{diagonal}}}{E_{\mathrm{full}}}\approx 91,\qquad
  \frac{E_{\mathrm{scalar}}}{E_{\mathrm{full}}}\approx 101,\qquad
  \frac{E_{\mathrm{rotated}}}{E_{\mathrm{full}}}\approx 111
\]
(Figure~\ref{fig:predict}b). The conclusion is one of the least trivial new
results of the paper:
\begin{quote}\itshape
Neither a scalar robustness rate, a coordinate-diagonal approximation, nor a
spectrum-preserving but eigendirection-scrambled control can reproduce the held-out
response. Predictive power resides in the full operator, including both eigenvalues
and eigendirections.
\end{quote}


\subsection{Stationary covariance and attribution}\label{sec:covclosure}
As a secondary internal-consistency check---not an independent cross-protocol
prediction---we ask whether the same frozen operator reproduces the stationary
covariance of a linear stochastic closure $A\Sigma+\Sigma A^\top=Q$ under a prescribed
driving $Q$. With a fixed anisotropic, correlated $Q$ ($\sigma_1{=}0.095$,
$\sigma_2{=}0.135$, $\rho{=}0.28$) the full operator reconstructs the covariance to
$1.65\%$ (Figure~\ref{fig:predict}c); but because $Q_{12}\neq0$ this
does not attribute the cross-covariance to $A$ alone.

\paragraph{Isotropic-forcing attribution.} The attribution is settled with \emph{isotropic} forcing
$Q=\sigma^2 I$: solving $A\Sigma+\Sigma A^\top=\sigma^2 I$ reproduces the exact-discrete
Ornstein--Uhlenbeck covariance to $1.08\%$, and because $Q_{12}=0$ the observed nonzero
cross-covariance $\Sigma_{12}\neq0$ is generated by the non-axis-aligned operator rather
than by correlated driving (scalar/diagonal/rotated controls fail by $54.21\%$,
$47.73\%$, $54.56\%$). The predicted and observed covariance matrices for the isotropic
case are listed in the Supplement; the correlated-driving matrices are included in the data
bundle. This is a constructed exact density-matrix/OU result that establishes
attribution within the model, not evidence that hardware noise obeys the closure; the
held-out load remains the primary cross-protocol prediction.

\section{Computing with $A^{-1}$}\label{sec:compute}

\subsection{Multistart convergence}
The identified operator is not only predictive but algorithmically useful. We compare
four descent updates on the same constructed landscape,
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \Delta n_{\mathrm{ordinary}}&=-\eta\,\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}}, &
  \Delta n_{\mathrm{scalar}}&=-\eta\,A_{\mathrm{scalar}}^{-1}\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}},\\
  \Delta n_{\mathrm{diag}}&=-\eta\,A_{\mathrm{diag}}^{-1}\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}}, &
  \Delta n_{\mathrm{full}}&=-\eta\,A^{-1}\nabla J_{\mathrm{loc}},
\end{aligned}
\]
where $A_{\mathrm{scalar}}$ and $A_{\mathrm{diag}}$ are the scalar and coordinate-diagonal
reductions of the full operator $A$. Across twenty random starts to a fixed
convergence tolerance, the full-operator preconditioner reaches the \emph{same} local
optimum with far fewer objective evaluations:
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{@{}l cccc@{}}
\toprule
Method & full-$A$ & diagonal & scalar & ordinary \\
\midrule
Mean evaluations  & $78.5$  & $165.0$  & $185.0$  & $201.5$  \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Evaluation counts include the initial objective call. The full-operator flow uses
$61.0\%$ fewer evaluations than ordinary gradient, while all four methods converge to the
\emph{same} local optimum. The V3 result is therefore a
\emph{convergence-efficiency advantage under the constructed landscape}---fewer objective
evaluations to the same optimum, not a lower asymptotic loss or higher asymptotic success.
Even charging the one-time identification cost entirely to a
single run leaves it well below ordinary gradient, and it amortizes across starts (Supplement).


\subsection{Equal-budget preconditioning}\label{sec:v4}
Under an equal budget of exactly $180$ evaluations ($30$ steps) for every optimizer,
the four flows reach:
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{@{}l cccc@{}}
\toprule
Method & full-$A$ & diagonal & scalar & ordinary \\
\midrule
$\lVert n_{\mathrm{final}}\rVert$     & $2.11\times10^{-13}$ & $5.54\times10^{-7}$ & $2.42\times10^{-6}$ & $5.73\times10^{-6}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
All four flows reach the same local optimum; the separating quantity is the residual
normal distance to the manifold below.

\subsection{Residual normal displacement and scope}
The clearer separation is the residual \emph{normal distance} to the stable--neutral
manifold, for which full-$A$ is orders of magnitude smaller
($\lVert n_{\mathrm{ordinary}}\rVert/\lVert n_{\mathrm{full}}\rVert\approx2.7\times10^{7}$,
scalar $\approx1.1\times10^{7}$, diagonal $\approx2.6\times10^{6}$); the full-$A$ Newton
flow reaches this near-quadratic optimum to numerical precision, so the ratio is limited by
the machine floor rather than being a fixed finite factor. Thus under an equal budget the
full-$A$ flow returns implementations far closer to the manifold, though it does not
materially raise success. Accordingly, the demonstrated advantage is convergence efficiency and
manifold proximity in the constructed landscape, not lower asymptotic loss, physical-QPU
robustness, or a higher asymptotic success probability.

\section{Supporting Robustness-Landscape Evidence}\label{sec:support}

We next report supporting exact-simulation evidence from path search, continuous basins, null
controls, and GHZ$_3$ recovery, on the path families of Section~\ref{sec:twoobj}.

\subsection{Path objective, framework, and protocols}\label{sec:framework}
The supporting benchmarks use endpoint-equivalent Bell and GHZ$_3$ path families under
coherent drift, depolarizing noise, and readout noise. A path objective combines a
mitigated total-variation term with a normalized implementation cost, subject to a hard
endpoint-equivalence constraint. Full simulation, objective, and search details---the
active-time cost ledger, the $R$ diagnostic, the geometric-flow relations, candidate
generation, and ablation policies---are given in the Supplement
(Section~\ref{sec:supp-framework}).

\subsection{Discrete pure search}
On a $54$-case grid (Bell and GHZ$_3$ under varied noise and drift; full settings in the
Supplement), a single-file validator returned \code{OVERALL\_PASS} (8/8). The pure search
selected non-shortest focused endpoint-equivalent paths in \emph{all} $54/54$ cases,
beating the shortest, long, and cost-only references, with the exact focus-echo path
recovered in $52/54$ and never handed the known references
(\code{observed\_exact\_reference}$=0/54$). The median TVD gain over the shortest
reference is $0.768$, endpoint equivalence holds to machine precision (max endpoint TVD
$=2.22\times10^{-16}$), and the per-flag counts and full policy table are in the
Supplement. Because $\lambda_R=0$ here, this experiment supports discovery of a
non-shortest focused endpoint-equivalent path, not an independent contribution from the
$R$/$K{=}1$ term.


\subsection{Continuous basin and null controls}
The discrete selection extends to a continuous one-parameter family: an endpoint-equivalent
implementation is parameterized by an echo angle $\alpha$ (physical circuit
$\text{prep}\to R_z(\phi/2)\to R_x(\alpha)\to R_z(\phi/2)\to R_x(-\alpha)\to\text{prep}^{-1}$),
so every $\alpha$ shares the same ideal endpoint, with $\alpha\approx\pi$ the $X$-echo that
refocuses the coherent $Z$ drift~\cite{hahn1950,meiboom1958,viola1999}. Across the tested families the minimum lay a median
$0.043$\,rad from $\pi$, and deterministic gradient~\cite{khaneja2005} and noisy
Langevin-type flows concentrated toward this basin (validator \code{OVERALL\_PASS}, 8/8).
The basin disappeared or reversed under no-drift, reverse-flow, and shuffled-landscape
controls (no-drift median gain falling from $0.769$ to $0.010$, focused fraction $2/18$);
full statistics and flow details are in the Supplement. Thus the focused basin is induced by
coherent-drift refocusing and the measured geometry of $J(\alpha)$
(Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:landscape}), not by the flow algorithm alone.

\subsection{GHZ$_3$ geometric error suppression}
The picture extends to an explicit-error recovery benchmark. On GHZ$_3$ under a known
coherent $Z$ phase error, a resource-matched geometric recovery path is compared against a
flat matched-recovery control, a random-phase ensemble, and a no-recovery baseline over
$6{,}300$ simulated circuit instances (paths $\times$ depths $\times$ noise levels $\times$
error angles $\times$ repeats; breakdown in the Supplement) at $4096$ shots each, with
endpoint equivalence audited on the matched recovery rows (error $\le 6.4\times10^{-14}$).

On the held-out test split the geometric recovery path attains mean success $0.7515$, versus
$0.3964$ for flat matched recovery, $0.4451$ for the random-phase median, and $0.4227$ for
no recovery (Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:error-suppression}); it also exceeds the best single random path
($0.6745$) and, on a hard subset, beats no-recovery by $+0.1432$. An audit over the raw
per-circuit outputs returns a full pass. This is recovery-like \emph{geometric
error-suppression}, \emph{not} logical quantum error correction: no syndrome, decoder,
code-distance scaling, or threshold.

\section{PASQAL Cloud EMU Evidence}\label{sec:pasqal}

\subsection{Area-matched BB1 ablation}\label{sec:pasqal-bb1}

We compared a BB1-type phase path with a nominally endpoint-equivalent flat control and
six resource-matched random-phase paths on PASQAL Cloud \code{EMU\_FREE}. All protocols
shared the pulse-area template $(\pi,\pi,2\pi,\pi)$, pulse number, duration
($1000$\,ns/pulse), backend, shot count ($500$), and measurement basis; only the phase
path differed, across four stress scales $\{0.6,0.8,1.0,1.2\}$. The random controls were
not assumed endpoint-equivalent.

The BB1-type and flat paths are nominally endpoint-equivalent up to a global phase (the
explicit unitary reduction is in the Supplement), so the BB1--flat comparison is the
primary endpoint-matched robustness test; the random-phase ensemble, whose ideal endpoint
is not generally matched, is a broader resource-matched negative control.

All $32/32$ jobs completed (no NaN, no error rows). Mean success was $0.9930$ for BB1,
$0.5000$ for the flat control, $0.5158$ for the random median, and $0.6725$ for the best
sampled random path (Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:pasqal}). Since the BB1 and flat paths are matched in
pulse number, area template, duration, backend, shots, and measurement basis, their
nearly twofold difference in mean success cannot be attributed to pulse area, duration,
backend, shot count, or measurement basis within the matched benchmark; the observed
difference is associated with the phase-path choice under these matched emulator
conditions. This is cloud-emulator evidence, not a physical-QPU run.

\subsection{Single-atom amplitude-response sweep}
As a separate cloud-stack response test, we executed a seven-point single-atom amplitude
sweep on PASQAL Cloud \code{EMU\_FREE}, with $200$ final bitstrings sampled per amplitude.
The measured excited-state response agreed with the analytic prediction
$p_1(A)=\sin^2(AT/2)$ with Pearson correlation $0.9924$, mean absolute error $0.0200$,
root-mean-square error $0.0264$, and matching sign of the local response slope. This experiment
is distinct from the area-matched BB1 ablation of Section~\ref{sec:pasqal-bb1} and does not
constitute physical-QPU validation.


\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection*{Interpretation}
The main insight is that executed path geometry matters even when ideal endpoints are
identical: two implementations that agree exactly in the noiseless limit can differ by an
order of magnitude in noisy TVD, and---more strongly---the transverse response of an
endpoint-equivalent family is organized by a full normal operator $A$ whose eigenvalues
\emph{and} eigendirections, not any scalar robustness score, carry the predictive and
algorithmic content. This shifts the object of study from ``how to build one good
circuit'' to ``how robustness is organized across the implementations that realize the
same ideal endpoint,'' and is complementary to gate-level optimization and quantum error
correction~\cite{shor1995,fowler2012} rather than a replacement. Whether this local
structure reflects a deeper geometric-flow paradigm remains a hypothesis; the contribution
here is to make that hypothesis operational and testable.

\subsection*{Scope and limitations}
\noindent\colorbox{boxbg}{\begin{minipage}{0.97\linewidth}
\vspace{2pt}
\textbf{Supported} (constructed exact density-matrix landscape, cross-checked against Qiskit/Aer): \emph{identify}---an
eigendirection-unseeded random-direction identification recovers the normal operator
($2.62\%$ Frobenius; alignment $>0.99999999$); \emph{predict}---a frozen $A$ predicts
held-out sustained load ($0.54\%$), beating scalar/diagonal/rotated reductions by
$91$--$111\times$; \emph{compute}---as $A^{-1}$ it is a more efficient preconditioner
($61\%$ fewer evaluations; orders of magnitude closer to the manifold at fixed budget). The
isotropic-driving covariance check ($1.08\%$) is secondary.
\textbf{Not supported (open):} the same advantages on a physical QPU; large
multi-qubit algorithms; any global-basin or global-manifold guarantee; that \emph{all}
endpoint-equivalent families are stable--neutral; that hardware time is generated by $A$
or that hardware noise obeys the OU/$Q$ closure; and any universal ``computation is
geometric flow'' or Born/Lorentzian claim.
\vspace{2pt}
\end{minipage}}

\medskip
Several limitations bound these claims.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Exact simulation, no QPU.}
The principal normal-operator results are produced by a self-contained exact
density-matrix propagator cross-checked against Qiskit/Aer, not by physical-QPU
measurements. The earlier direct-Aer outputs are retained only as a historical
implementation cross-check. All PASQAL results are author-run Cloud
\code{EMU\_FREE} results, not physical-QPU measurements or independent
third-party replications; the reported normal operator and covariance are
simulation-derived rather than hardware-tomographic quantities.
\item \emph{Small, low-dimensional systems.} Only Bell/GHZ$_3$ landscape families and a two-dimensional normal space (single working qubit) are tested; the V5.1 identification statistics are reported at a fixed random seed.
\item \emph{Constructed local loss.} The normal-operator results use a separate constructed loss $J_{\mathrm{loc}}$ (Section~\ref{sec:construct}), not the $54$-case path objective $J_{\mathrm{path}}$; $A$ is the metric-raised normal Hessian in the Euclidean chart $g=I$, so the preconditioning advantage is stated for this parameterization.
\item \emph{Local, not global, geometry.} Stable--neutrality is verified locally for one family and is not asserted for all endpoint-equivalent families; the global topology and smoothness of $\mathcal M_{\mathrm{eq}}$---whether a single smooth component or a stratified manifold---are not established, and a supplementary output-QFI study confirms local geometry does not by itself fix global basin topology (Supplement).
\item \emph{Prescribed OU closure.} The stationary covariance uses a prescribed driving $Q$ (correlated, then isotropic $Q=\sigma^2 I$); attribution of $\Sigma_{12}\neq0$ to the operator holds \emph{within the model}. The V3/V4 advantage is convergence efficiency and manifold proximity, not lower asymptotic loss or robust drift-recovery dominance.
\item \emph{No QEC, threshold, or universal ontology.} The GHZ$_3$ benchmark is recovery-like suppression of explicit coherent errors, not logical quantum error correction; BB1 is a robustness landmark, not a new pulse; and the $K{=}1$/Lorentzian/Born/OU motivation (Supplement) and the ``computation is geometric flow'' reading are hypotheses, not results.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Outlook}
Natural next steps are validation on a physical PASQAL \textsc{FRESNEL} QPU, extension to
higher-dimensional normal spaces and multi-qubit families, hardware-estimated Hessians, and
broader independent-driving covariance sweeps across multiple amplitudes and normal-space
dimensions.
The most accurate summary is that the full normal operator is measurable, cross-protocol
predictive, and algorithmically useful in a controlled implementation-space realization;
whether this extends to hardware and to a universal geometric-flow paradigm is open.

% =====================================================================

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
We constructed a local stable--neutral implementation geometry whose transverse response
is governed by a full normal operator $A$. In exact density-matrix simulation, the normal
operator was estimated by two routes---a local quadratic fit and a random-direction
trajectory reconstruction---with close agreement in spectrum and eigendirections. The
frozen quadratic-fit estimate then predicted held-out sustained response substantially
better than scalar, diagonal, and eigendirection-scrambled reductions.

Using $A^{-1}$ as a preconditioner reduced the evaluation count to the same local optimum
by $61.0\%$ and left a residual normal displacement orders of magnitude smaller under a fixed
budget. Thus the same operator can be identified, used predictively, and inverted
computationally:
\[
  \text{identify }A\ \to\ \text{predict with }A\ \to\ \text{compute with }A^{-1}.
\]

These results establish a local constructive proof of principle, supported by
robustness-landscape and PASQAL Cloud EMU evidence. They do not constitute physical-QPU
validation or a universal claim that computation is geometric flow.

\subsection*{Path-landscape framework and protocols (details)}\label{sec:supp-framework}
\paragraph{Implementation-manifold flow.}
For a fixed ideal task endpoint we study explicitly parameterized families of
task-endpoint-equivalent implementations (Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:paradigm}). Two
implementations are called \emph{task-endpoint-equivalent} when, for the fixed
input state and measurement used in the benchmark, their ideal noiseless output
distributions coincide within the prescribed tolerance; this is weaker than
equality of the implemented unitary or quantum channel. Let $\Theta$ denote a
chosen parameter space of physical implementations, and define the admissible
endpoint-equivalent set
\[
  \Theta_{p_\star}^{(\varepsilon)}
  =
  \left\{
  \theta\in\Theta:\
  \mathrm{TVD}\!\left(p_{\mathrm{ideal}}(\theta),p_\star\right)
  \le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{end}}
  \right\}.
\]
Within locally smooth, constant-rank regions this set is modeled as an
implementation manifold; globally it may be disconnected, bounded, singular, or
stratified, and we do not assume it forms a single smooth manifold. A point
$\theta$ labels one physical realization, represented by a control trajectory
$\Gamma_\theta$; a curve $s\mapsto\theta_s$ describes a continuous deformation or
search between implementations, and is distinct from the physical time evolution
along $\Gamma_\theta$. Noise induces a measured robustness landscape
\[
  J:\Theta_{p_\star}^{(\varepsilon)}\to\mathbb{R},
\]
where lower $J$ means lower noisy error at the fixed ideal task endpoint. The
geometric-flow hypothesis is that robust implementations are organized by descent
and noisy concentration in \emph{implementation space},
\[
  d\theta_s
  =
  -\operatorname{grad}_{g}J(\theta_s)\,ds
  +
  \sqrt{2D}\,dB_s^{g},
\]
a flow over implementation parameters, not the physical time evolution of the
hardware. In the present experiments, $\theta$ is instantiated by discrete echo
paths, by the continuous coordinate $\alpha$, and by area- and duration-matched
phase paths in the PASQAL Cloud EMU ablation. The tests below ask whether $J$
contains a focused basin, whether flow concentrates toward it, and whether the
basin collapses under null controls.

\paragraph{Endpoint-equivalent paths.}
Each candidate path is realized as two circuits over the same qubits: a \emph{control-endpoint} circuit that fixes the ideal endpoint, and a \emph{physical} circuit that additionally contains the drift and echo blocks. An exact statevector simulation of the control-endpoint circuit gives the ideal target; the endpoint TVD between a candidate's control-endpoint distribution and the target measures deviation from endpoint equivalence. Here the modeled coherent drift, stochastic gate noise, and readout noise are treated as error mechanisms and are set to zero in the definition of $p_{\mathrm{ideal}}$. Task-endpoint equivalence is therefore audited on the corresponding error-free control circuit, whereas the physical circuit reinstates these error mechanisms to measure robustness.


\paragraph{Active-time cost ledger.}
Each executed physical circuit is scored by a fixed per-gate ledger (e.g.\ single-qubit gates $1.0$, \code{rz} $0.35$, \code{id} $0.25$, \code{cx} $4.0$). The summed weight is the executed cost; it is normalized by the long-reference cost. This ledger is an accounting device for path length, not a physical time measurement.


\paragraph{Objective and $R$ diagnostic.}
For probability distributions $p,q$ we write the total-variation distance
\[
  \mathrm{TVD}(p,q)=\tfrac12\sum_x\lvert p_x-q_x\rvert .
\]
Tensored-inverse readout mitigation can return a quasi-distribution with negative
entries or unit-sum violations; the mitigated vector is therefore clipped to
nonnegative entries and renormalized,
\[
  \tilde p_x=\frac{\max(\hat p_x,0)}{\sum_y\max(\hat p_y,0)},
\]
and all reported mitigated TVDs are computed from $\tilde p$, so
$\mathrm{TVD}_{\text{mit}}$ is always a distance between genuine distributions.
(This clip-and-renormalize map is not the Euclidean projection onto the simplex,
and is not described as such.) Candidates are scored by
\[
  J = \mathrm{TVD}_{\text{mit}} + \lambda_{\text{cost}}\cdot\text{cost}_{\text{norm}}
      + \lambda_R\,|R-1| + \text{endpoint\_penalty},
\]
where $\mathrm{TVD}_{\text{mit}}$ is the total-variation distance to the target~\cite{nielsen2010} after readout mitigation, $\text{cost}_{\text{norm}}$ is the normalized executed cost, and the endpoint term enforces endpoint equivalence as a hard admissibility constraint,
\[
  \Pi_{\text{end}}(\theta)=
  \begin{cases}
    0, & d_{\text{end}}(\theta)\le\varepsilon_{\text{end}},\\
    +\infty, & d_{\text{end}}(\theta)>\varepsilon_{\text{end}},
  \end{cases}
  \qquad
  d_{\text{end}}(\theta)=\mathrm{TVD}\!\left(p_{\text{ideal}}(\theta),p_\star\right),
  \quad \varepsilon_{\text{end}}=10^{-10}.
\]
In the numerical implementation this constraint is realized as an admissibility gate---a candidate whose endpoint TVD exceeds $\varepsilon_{\text{end}}$ is discarded rather than penalized---so no finite penalty constant enters the reported $J$. The ratio $R$ is a bookkeeping diagnostic: $R_{\text{matched}}=1$ when the executed ledger matches the reference scale and $R_{\text{wrong}}=1.25$ under a deliberately mismatched scale. In this study $\lambda_R=0$, so $R$ is reported as a diagnostic only and does not drive selection.


\paragraph{Geometric-flow framework: four defining relations.}
The framework is defined by four relations that together characterize the geometry of the implementation manifold. Let $p^{(\alpha)}_{\text{ideal}}$ be the ideal (noiseless) endpoint distribution of the implementation with echo coordinate $\alpha$, $p_{\star}$ the reference endpoint, $\hat p^{(\alpha)}$ the raw readout-mitigated quasi-distribution, and $\tilde p^{(\alpha)}$ its clipped and renormalized probability distribution.

\emph{Relation 1 (Endpoint equivalence).} Every implementation shares the same ideal endpoint,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{TVD}\!\left(p^{(\alpha)}_{\text{ideal}},\, p_{\star}\right) \;\le\; \varepsilon_{\text{end}}, \qquad \varepsilon_{\text{end}} = 10^{-10},
\end{equation}
witnessed here at machine precision ($\le 4.44\times10^{-16}$).

\emph{Relation 2 (Selection objective).} Implementations are ranked by
\begin{equation}
  J(\alpha) \;=\; \mathrm{TVD}\!\left(\tilde p^{(\alpha)},\, p_{\star}\right) + \lambda_{\text{cost}}\,\hat c(\alpha) + \lambda_R\,\lvert R-1\rvert + \Pi_{\text{end}}(\alpha),
\end{equation}
with $\hat c$ the normalized executed cost, $R$ the ledger diagnostic, and $\Pi_{\text{end}}$ the endpoint penalty ($\Pi_{\text{end}}=0$ when Relation~1 holds).

\emph{Relation 3 (Focused basin).} The measured landscape is non-monotone in $\alpha$. Writing the periodic distance on the echo circle $S^1$ as $d_{S^1}(\alpha,\beta)=\min_{k\in\mathbb Z}\lvert\alpha-\beta+2\pi k\rvert$, the objective attains a minimizer
\begin{equation}
  \alpha^{\star}\in\operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\alpha\in\mathcal A}\, J(\alpha),
  \qquad d_{S^1}(\alpha^{\star},\pi)\ll 1 ,
  \label{eq:focused-basin}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ the minimizer sits near $\alpha=\pi$ (measured median $d_{S^1}(\alpha^{\star},\pi)=0.043$ rad). The landscape is non-monotone because the coherent residual depends nonlinearly on the echo angle: the focused value $\alpha\approx\pi$ approximately cancels the two drift halves, whereas $\alpha\approx0$ leaves the drift unrefocused. The location and shape of the minimum are therefore reported as properties of the measured landscape rather than derived from an angle-dependent exposure model. Equation~\eqref{eq:focused-basin} is an operational description of the measured $J(\alpha)$, not a derived closed form.

\emph{Relation 4 (Robustness gain).} For a comparator $c$, define the absolute
success gain
\begin{equation}
  \Delta S_c \;=\; S_{\text{geo}} - S_c .
\end{equation}
When $S_c>0$, the corresponding relative gain is
\begin{equation}
  G_c \;=\; \frac{S_{\text{geo}} - S_c}{S_c}.
\end{equation}
The endpoint-matched quantity $\Delta S_{\text{flat}}$ is the primary robustness
diagnostic---absolute gain is used because some matched flat controls can
collapse to zero success at individual stress points. Gains relative to the
random-phase controls are reported as descriptive resource-matched comparisons,
because those controls are not generally task-endpoint-equivalent. The absolute
gain is the primary reported diagnostic; the relative gain is defined for
completeness (Section~\ref{sec:pasqal}).

\paragraph{Simulation methods.}\label{sec:methods}
Simulations use Qiskit/Aer~\cite{qiskit} with a depolarizing plus readout noise model ($p_1=0.0015$, $p_2=0.012$, $p_{\text{id}}=0.001$, $p_{\text{readout}}=0.025$, scaled by a per-case noise factor). Test families are Bell (2 qubits, success $\ket{00}$) and GHZ$_3$ (3 qubits, success $\ket{000}$)~\cite{bell1964,ghsz1990}. Each physical circuit prepares the state, applies a coherent global $Z$ drift over \code{drift\_blocks}$=12$ blocks with optional $X$-echo focusing/overexposure, then applies the inverse preparation and measures. Readout error is corrected by an independent tensored-inverse mitigation~\cite{temme2017,kandala2019}. Reference paths are: shortest (no echo), focused (one echo cycle), and long-overexposed (eight echo cycles, $32$ idle identities). All randomness is seeded ($\text{seed}=260709$).


\paragraph{Candidate generation.}
A candidate is specified by: \code{family} (Bell or GHZ$_3$), \code{echo\_mode} $\in\{$\code{none}, \code{focus\_echo}, \code{over\_echo}$\}$, \code{placement} $\in\{$\code{after\_entangle}, \code{before\_uncompute}, \code{interleaved}$\}$, \code{echo\_cycles}, \code{idle\_ids} (idle-identity count), and a phase \code{theta}. Proposals are drawn from a biased-but-non-forced distribution that favors low echo/idle exposure but keeps enough focus candidates for discovery.


\paragraph{Objective and pure-search protocol.}
Each pooled candidate is evaluated once (calibration shots) and scored by $J$. Under the \emph{pure-search} protocol the shortest, focused-medium, and long reference paths are \textbf{not} inserted into the pool; they are constructed and measured only in the validation phase for comparison. We record whether each exact reference was ever proposed by the search (\code{observed\_exact\_*\_reference}).


\paragraph{Ablation policies.}
From the observed calibration pool we select one candidate per policy and re-measure it at higher validation shots. The policies are: \code{k1\_score} (minimize $J$), \code{tvd\_only\_mitigated}, \code{tvd\_only\_noisy}, \code{cost\_only}, \code{random\_observed}, \code{oracle\_observed}, plus the three fixed references \code{shortest\_reference}, \code{medium\_reference}, \code{long\_reference}.

Crucially, the search protocol is not the claim. The claim is about the discovered path: a path with non-trivial phase geometry that maintains robustness under stress, which the area-matched flat and random-phase controls do not. The search algorithm is a tool, not the message. We include it to demonstrate that such robust paths are discoverable in the landscape without prior knowledge; the robustness of the discovered path is then verified by direct measurement (the ablation policies and null controls in Section~\ref{sec:support}, and the area-matched controls of Section~\ref{sec:pasqal}). This discoverability statement applies to the Qiskit/Aer pure-search benchmark. In the PASQAL Cloud EMU study, the BB1-type path is not discovered by the random-phase search; it is inserted as a known robustness landmark and compared against flat and random area-matched phase controls.

\subsection*{PASQAL BB1: endpoint equivalence and gains}
At unit stress the flat sequence has all rotation axes aligned, so
$U_{\mathrm{flat}}=R_x(5\pi)\sim R_x(\pi)$; for the BB1-type sequence the three-pulse
correction block satisfies $R_{\varphi}(\pi)\,R_{3\varphi}(2\pi)\,R_{\varphi}(\pi)\sim I$
with $\varphi=\arccos(-1/4)$, so $U_{\mathrm{BB1}}\sim R_x(\pi)$ as well, though the
intermediate phase paths differ. In the absolute-gain diagnostic (Relation~4) the success
gains are $\Delta S_{\mathrm{flat}}=0.4930$ (endpoint-matched primary),
$\Delta S_{\mathrm{rand,med}}=0.4773$, and $\Delta S_{\mathrm{rand,best}}=0.3205$; the two
random-phase gains are descriptive resource-matched comparisons, since those controls are
not generally task-endpoint-equivalent. Part of the low random-path success may reflect a
different ideal target rather than robustness alone.

\subsection*{Boundary test: local geometry does not fix global basin structure}
A supplementary four-dimensional output-QFI study (across $1{,}920$ matched simulator
runs) supports the importance of directional alignment---output-QFI flow improves
held-out cost and convergence over Euclidean and spectrum-matched scrambled-QFI
controls---but also shows that local geometry does not determine global basin topology or
guarantee uniform convergence: QFI failure rates under a matched paired-regret criterion
range up to $8.33\%$ across noise regimes, with no single local diagnostic predicting the
residual failures. The full matched-run table, confidence intervals, regime-normalized
failure rates, and diagnostic correlations accompany the reproduction bundle.

\subsection*{Interpretive motivation, not tested here}
The design was originally motivated by a $K{=}1$ Lorentzian ``active-time'' picture with a
Born-type fixed point and an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck~\cite{uhlenbeck1930} view of residual
flow, and by the geometric quantum computation tradition~\cite{zanardi1999}. That language
is used only as a scoring intuition for active-time path ledgers and belongs to the
paradigm-level reading this paper treats as a hypothesis. \textbf{The present experiments
do not show that hardware dynamics are Lorentzian, nor do they prove Born fixed-point
convergence or OU residual-flow theory.} No quantitative result in this paper depends on
that interpretation; it is recorded only to document the origin of the scoring intuition.
